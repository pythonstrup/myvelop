## Development

### 1\. One Commit a Day

Once again, I successfully maintained my one-commit-a-day streak throughout the year.

It no longer feels like, "I develop because I have to make one commit a day." Instead, it feels more like, "I develop every day, and one commit a day naturally records that process." It is almost like keeping a diary for development. I see this as a clearly positive change.

![One commit a day](../../../assets/blog/1/01.png)

### 2\. The Blog

I enjoyed my experience in Geultto Cohort 9, so I joined Cohort 10 as well.

[Geultto Cohort 10 Begins!](https://myvelop.tistory.com/244)

Since joining Geultto, I have spent much more time thinking about how to write better posts.

I believe the quality of my writing improved as a result, but contrary to my expectations, blog traffic actually fell by half. The growth of AI may have reduced the number of people who search for information themselves, but I believe the biggest reason ultimately lies with me.

There is a lot I want to write about, yet turning those ideas into finished posts takes a long time. My greatest regret is that I could not produce more content.

### 3\. Open Source Contributions

Ever since I started developing software, one of my goals has been to contribute to an open source project that almost everyone would recognize by name.

That was when the <strong>Open Source Contribution Academy</strong> caught my attention.

[The 2024 Open Source Contribution Academy Begins!](https://myvelop.tistory.com/232)

I primarily worked on automating updates for <strong>Helium</strong>, Zeppelin's dependency management tool. It had previously been managed with S3 and AWS Lambda, but AWS server support was discontinued, so we needed to migrate to a new approach. To address this, I worked on <strong>automation using GitHub Actions</strong>. Along the way, I found and fixed bugs in Helium and also helped containerize it with Docker to support Kubernetes (k8s).

Making even a single contribution took longer than I had expected. The maintainers were working developers rather than full-time contributors, so after opening a PR, it was common to wait <strong>more than a week</strong> for a review.

I could not devote all my time to open source contributions because of personal study-group commitments and other obligations, but I made sure to attend the <strong>offline meetings held every weekend</strong>. I enjoyed sharing meals and talking about development with everyone, and I learned a great deal through the many books they recommended.

We also achieved a meaningful result. After our team's contributions, <strong>Apache Zeppelin, which includes Helium, earned the highest community health score among Apache Top-Level Projects (TLPs), including Kafka and Spark</strong>. It scored a perfect <strong>10 out of 10!</strong> 🎉

![Apache Foundation community health score](../../../assets/blog/1/02.png)

We were also told that User Discussion activity traffic rose by 246%, while PR Review activity traffic increased by 858%.

![User Discussion graph](../../../assets/blog/1/03.png)

![PR Review activity traffic graph](../../../assets/blog/1/04.png)

In recognition of these accomplishments, we received an Excellence Award. I had never imagined we would win such a major award, so I was both surprised and delighted.

![Open Source Contribution Excellence Award](../../../assets/blog/1/05.png)

### 4\. Toss Runner's High, Cohort 1

<strong>What is the essence of our work?</strong> Ultimately, it is <strong>making the product successful</strong>.

Product success, however, does not come from writing good code alone. Moving in the right direction requires <strong>data-driven communication</strong>. Rather than fearing failure, we need to <strong>try bold ideas and create results through the process</strong>.

Through Toss mentoring, I learned how to explain the need for change logically and communicate more clearly how improved outcomes affect the organization. I used to be someone who said, "Wouldn't this probably be better?" Now, I have become <strong>someone who can persuade teammates with concrete evidence</strong>.

I applied for Cohort 25 in the summer, but naturally, an application thrown together on the evening of the deadline was not enough to get accepted.

## A New Challenge: PM

As soon as I joined Nexters for the first time, I embarked on the reckless challenge of taking on the <strong>PM role</strong>.

### As a Backend Developer

This was a chance to see the results of my efforts. <strong>I realized that my two years of professional experience had not gone to waste.</strong> I had become someone who could understand the flow of a development project and <strong>coordinate and lead the schedules of both backend and frontend teams</strong>.

In fact, <strong>I spent almost no time writing backend code myself.</strong> Instead, I designed the overall project, defined domain models for the team, and used code reviews to guide the direction of development. <s>Isn't this basically a CTO?</s>

The backend teammates showed <strong>incredible enthusiasm.</strong> They had many questions and were more proactive than anyone in communicating. PRs accumulated dozens of comments, and I learned a lot through the process as well. <strong>Discovering gaps in my own knowledge, reading other people's code, and discussing it together was genuinely enjoyable.</strong>

![Code review 1](../../../assets/blog/1/06.png)

![Code review 2](../../../assets/blog/1/07.png)

### As a PM

I genuinely cared about the product's success and jumped into anything I could help with, regardless of the task.

During one round of user research, I <strong>declared to the team that I would collect responses from at least 50 people</strong>. I approached coworkers one by one to ask them to complete the survey and reached out to friends as well. That still did not feel like enough, so I posted the following message in Geultto's open promotion channel to spread the word further.

![Promoting the survey in Geultto](../../../assets/blog/1/08.png)

Ultimately, we received <strong>103 survey responses in just 48 hours</strong>, which gave us meaningful data for our research.

![User research results](../../../assets/blog/1/09.png)

At work, I handle some planning responsibilities in addition to development, and that experience helped me significantly in the PM role. One teammate even told me I seemed more like a planner than a backend developer. <s>The designers might disagree.</s>

I am doing everything I can to bring the project to a successful conclusion. I <strong>estimate and present schedules</strong>, communicate actively with the team, and take responsibility for <strong>identifying and resolving current bottlenecks</strong>. To keep us as close to the schedule as possible, I am now working not only on the backend but also on <strong>frontend and Flutter development</strong>, which keeps me very busy.

### As a Leader

I believe I fell short as a leader in many ways.

When a leader is capable, the team does not have to struggle as much. Instead, my shortcomings made things harder for my teammates, and that is what I regret most.

I believe a good leader is someone who creates the highest possible work efficiency. By efficiency, I do not mean demanding sacrifices from teammates. What matters is <strong>minimizing opportunity cost</strong> through sound decisions and <strong>preventing bottlenecks between disciplines</strong> through proper schedule coordination.

That requires <strong>strategy and planning</strong>. A leader must <strong>think several moves ahead of the team</strong> to offer a more effective direction and reduce wasted effort. These abilities become especially important when time is limited.
